The problem

Namma Yatri is an open-network ride-hailing platform operating across Bengaluru and other Indian cities on the ONDC stack. Unlike Ola or Uber, it runs on a zero-commission model where drivers keep 100% of the fare.

The Datathon provided four raw CSVs spanning the full rider journey: search requests, driver quotes, bookings, and cancellations. The task was to identify where the platform loses riders, understand driver cancellation behaviour, and surface actionable recommendations. Two questions had to be answered in 2.5 hours.

The two questions

Q1: Analyse the conversion funnel from search to completed trip. Segment by time of day and trip length. Identify the biggest drop-off points and hypothesise why.

Q2: Analyse booking cancellations. Who cancels, why, and what patterns emerge across driver ratings, pickup distance, and time of day?

Before any analysis, I mapped how the four tables connect. Each table represents one stage of the rider journey and links to the next via a shared key.

Key Structural Finding

The platform loses 47.2% of all demand at the very first step — nearly 1 in 2 searches never receives a driver quote. This is a supply-side failure, not a UX or pricing failure. Once a rider receives a quote, they book at an 83.7% rate, proving the product and fare are acceptable. The two problem areas are upstream (quote generation) and downstream (post-booking cancellations).

Hypotheses and validation
H1 — Driver supply is insufficient at peak Evening hours
Evidence: Evening has 40% of all search volume (2,528 searches) but the lowest Search→Quote rate at 42.3% — a 26pp gap vs Day. The platform is being flooded with demand while driver supply does not scale proportionally. Verdict: Supported.
H2 — Drivers selectively avoid short trips due to low fare economics
Evidence: Short trips receive 0.70 quotes per search vs 3.96 for Long trips. 58.4% of short trip searches receive zero quotes. A driver 2km away accepting a 3km trip earns less than their fuel cost for the round-trip, creating a structural disincentive. Verdict: Supported.
H3 — Quote-to-Booking is consistently high, so fare and UX are not the problem
Evidence: Q→B rates range from 76.9% (Night) to 86.0% (Medium trips) — a tight band across all segments. Once a rider sees a quote, they almost always book. This means product focus should target upstream (quote generation) and downstream (post-booking cancellations). Verdict: Supported.

Rating is a weak signal

The 4.5–5 bucket has the lowest cancellation rate (7%) but accounts for 76% of all cancellations in absolute terms simply because most drivers fall in this range. A driver's average star rating is an unreliable predictor of cancellation risk. A more reliable signal would be an accept-to-complete ratio tracked per driver over a rolling window, flagging drivers who chronically accept and cancel regardless of their average rating.

05
Recommendations
01
Dynamic supply incentives for peak Evening hours
Search→Quote drops to 42.3% in Evening despite 40% of all volume occurring then. Surge pricing visibility, destination filter nudges, or guaranteed-fare mechanics could unlock driver supply at 5–9 PM where the gap is largest.
02
Short-trip fare rebalancing or demand aggregation
Short trips receive 0.70 quotes per search — 5.6× fewer than Long trips. Restructure fare minimums for short trips to improve driver economics, or explore pooling to batch short trips together and increase per-driver payout.
03
In-app rider reachability features
NOT_REACHABLE is the #1 driver cancellation reason (212 cases). Auto-notifications to riders when a driver is 2–3 minutes away, plus in-app call shortcuts with one tap, would materially reduce this category.
04
Pickup location confirmation at booking
PICKUP_LOC_TOO_FAR (141 cases) spikes in Evening. Showing the driver's distance to the displayed pickup point before confirmation — with a one-tap correction — would reduce this reason significantly.
05
Accept-quality score (beyond star rating)
Star ratings show only a 0.04 median gap between cancellers and non-cancellers. Build a rolling accept-to-complete ratio per driver and use it to deprioritise high-cancellation drivers in quote dispatch during peak hours.
